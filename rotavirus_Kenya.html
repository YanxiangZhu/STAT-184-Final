Rotavirus Survey in Nairobi Kenya

Rotavirus is the most common cause of severe gastroenteritis among children worldwide. In 2013 WHO estimated that there was about 215,000 deaths from Rotavirus. This is lower from the estimated 528,000 deaths from rotavirus in 2010. Most of this deeaths occur in the Sub saharan Africa. In 2013 India, Nigeria, Democratic Republic of Congo and Pakistan accounted for about 49% of this deaths. Although vaccination is the most effective way of combacting rotavirus, poor countries are often affected due to lack of good health care.

In Kenya rotavirus is estimated to cause 3908 deaths, 3015 outpatient visits and 279 hospitalisation per 100,000 children under the age of 5. It is also estimated that this costs the kenyan economy $11 million. In 2014 the government of Kenya introduced a routine rotavirus immunisation. The vaccine is now available to the public for free through public hospitals. This is a big milestone for kenya as the World Bank estimates that 40% of Kenyans live under a dollar per day. The cost of one dose of Rotavirus vaccine through private hospitals is about $50. This is shows that almoast half of the population might have been unable to afford this vaccine.

After the introduction of this vaccine in public hospitals several studies have been conducted to find out the impact of the vaccine. The main study conducted is done by Kenya Medical Research Institue, Center for Disease Control(United States of America) and funded by Bill and Melinda Gates foundation. The research is expected to end by the end of 2017.

In December 2016 Mbagathi hospital carried out a research on the factors that influenced parents to vaccinate their children. I will use this data set to try and see what factors Influence vaccination in Kenya. The data set is available in both excel, SPSS and csv format.

A logistic regression was fitted to see how education, distance from a health center, maternal age, maternal knowledge about vaccination affect vaccination. Of the four variables a mothers knowledge about rotavirus is the best determinant of whether a parent had immunized her child. Infact a 1 point increase in a mothers knowledge increases the chances of vaccination by 18%. Maternal age was also significant. Older parents are likely to have immunised there children compared to the younger ones. This might be as a result of experience. It might also be a social economic status. Education did not have an effect on whether a parent has immunized her child, neither did distance. Incase of distance, given that this is the capital of Kenya, it is developed than the rural areas, hence we expect that there are health facilities nearby. In rural settings where sometimes people have to walk miles to find a health facility distance from a health facility might have played a role.

From the tree model, we can see that those who had a knowledge score of 10.5 and above, over 90% had immunized their children. From a sample of 350, 150 of them had scored 10.5 and above and in that group almost all of them had immunized their children. This shows how effective educating parents about benefits of vaccination and disease symptoms can improve percentage of immunized population in Sub Saharan Africa. The next important variable from the tree model is maternal age, Mothers who are 28 years and older have higher percentage of immunized children than their younger counterparts.
